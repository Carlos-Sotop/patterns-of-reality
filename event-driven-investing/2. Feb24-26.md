### Student Loans: The Elephant in the Room

This is the most dramatic deterioration across all credit categories:

- **25% of borrowers with a payment due are now delinquent** — nearly 3x the pre-pandemic rate
- **7.9 million borrowers** entered delinquency in the first 3 quarters of 2025 alone
- **9 million borrowers** (1 in 5) are in default — **largest number on record**
- Delinquent borrowers' credit scores dropped **57 points on average**, plunging 75% into deep subprime
- **Projection:** 13 million borrowers could be in default by end of 2026; if SAVE forbearance borrowers delinquent at the same rate, total distress could reach 17 million
- **Worst states:** New Mexico (29.6%), Oklahoma (28.2%), West Virginia (27.8%)

**Why This Matters for Markets:** Student loan defaults don't exist in a vacuum. Borrowers with 57-point credit score drops are being cut off from auto loans, credit cards, and mortgages. This is a **credit multiplier** — each student loan default creates knock-on effects across every other credit category.

### Auto Loans: Elevated but Stabilizing

- 30+ day delinquency rate: **3.88%** (Q3 2025)
- TransUnion projects 60+ day to rise only 3bps to 1.54% by Q4 2026
- Subprime portfolios remain under pressure
- Average monthly payments jumped ~30% (2020-2023): $470 → $600
- Rate of deterioration is slowing — not the epicenter, but symptomatic

### Private Credit: The Shadow Stress

Private credit is the market's blind spot — opaque, illiquid, and increasingly fragile:

- **True default rate (including selective defaults):** approaching **5%** — 2.5x the headline rate
- **40% of private credit borrowers have negative free cash flow** (up from 25% in 2021)
- **10% of borrowers operating under covenant relief** (up from 9%)
- DOJ has warned about "creative" marks and divergent valuation practices
- UBS projects defaults could climb by **3 percentage points** in 2026
- **Despite all this:** the industry continues to raise billions. Investors are dismissing "cockroach" warnings

**The Reflexivity Problem:** Private credit funds mark their own books. When defaults rise, managers delay recognition to avoid redemptions. This creates a gap between *reported* and *actual* stress — until it snaps. The longer the gap persists, the more violent the correction.
