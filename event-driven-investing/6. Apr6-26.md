# The Second Front | April 6, 2026

Day 37 of war. No ceasefire. Hormuz at 5% throughput. Brent $109. Gold recovering. And on April 2 -- the anniversary of Liberation Day -- Trump opened a second front: 100% tariffs on branded pharmaceuticals. The market's worst day since 2020.

The tariff escalation compounds with the war economy. Two independent inflationary forces now squeeze supply chains and pricing power simultaneously.

## What Happened

**War.** Trump said on April 1 that Iran's president requested a ceasefire. Iran denied it. Trump's condition: Hormuz must reopen first. Iran's condition: all strikes must stop first. Neither can concede without appearing to capitulate. Shipping analysts assess routine Hormuz transit is unlikely to resume for the remainder of 2026.

Transit is at roughly 6 vessels per day, down from 130 pre-war. The UK convened 35 nations on April 1 to discuss reopening the strait. The $20B DFC reinsurance program we flagged in entry 5 still lacks P&I liability coverage. The insurance gap -- not the missiles -- remains the binding constraint.

**Pharma tariffs.** On April 2, Trump invoked Section 232 to impose 100% tariffs on patented pharmaceutical imports, with a 20% rate for companies committing to US manufacturing and 0% for those signing MFN pricing agreements with HHS. Country-specific deals: 15% for EU, Japan, Korea, Switzerland; 10% for UK. Takes effect in 120-180 days.

Liberation Day playbook: shock tariff, negotiate down, extract concessions. But the timing compounds with the Hormuz crisis. Entry 5's blind spot #6 -- 47% of US generic drugs route through Gulf petrochemical supply chains -- identified a 4-6 week buffer before drug shortages. That buffer began depleting in early March. Branded drugs now face tariff-driven price shocks while generics face supply-driven shortages. Two independent forces, same sector, opposite ends.

**USDA Prospective Plantings (March 31).** Corn: 95.3M acres, down 3%. Soybeans: 84.7M acres, up 4%. Confirms the March 9 fertilizer-to-food thesis. Gulf nitrogen suppliers offline, farmers substituted toward soybeans (which fix their own nitrogen), acreage shift locked in. NASS noted geopolitical tensions during the survey window directly affected farmer decisions. Irreversible. Reduced corn supply tightens Q4 2026 into 2027.

CF Industries at $130, up 71% YTD, #1 in the S&P 500 for the second consecutive month.

## The Tariff-War Compound

The March 22 contradiction audit reduced war-continuation concentration from 65% to 35%. The pharma tariffs arrived on a different vector entirely -- no shared catalyst with the war. But both compound through the same transmission: consumer price inflation.

The consumer is now absorbing:
1. Oil at $109 (diesel $5.07, trucking +71%)
2. 10-15% tariff pass-through on most imported goods
3. 100% tariff on branded pharmaceuticals (120-180 day fuse)
4. Existing credit stress (4.8% delinquency rate, 25% student loan delinquency)
5. Tightening financial conditions (10Y at 4.39%, HY at 470 bps)

Together these create a consumption squeeze that no single policy response can relieve. The inflationary forces (tariffs, oil) contradict the deflationary response (rate cuts). The Fed cannot cut because inflation is rising. The Fed cannot hold because the consumer is breaking.

## What the Market Is Not Pricing

**1. The pharmaceutical-generic pincer.**

Branded drugs face 100% tariffs (120-180 day fuse). Generic drugs face supply disruption from Gulf petrochemical feedstock shortages (4-6 week buffer, now 5 weeks in). These are independent supply shocks hitting the same sector from opposite ends. No sell-side analyst covers both tariff policy and Gulf petrochemical logistics.

Branded drug prices rise from tariffs. Generic drug supply contracts from Hormuz disruption. Hospital systems that substituted generics for branded drugs to control costs now face shortages of the substitutes. Not in any pharmaceutical company's forward guidance.

**2. WTI-Brent inversion.**

WTI crude is trading at a premium to Brent -- a rare structural inversion. Normally Brent trades $3-5 above WTI because it reflects global (including Gulf) pricing. The inversion signals that US domestic crude (WTI) is more valuable than waterborne crude (Brent) because the physical delivery infrastructure for Gulf-sourced barrels is broken. Refiners with access to US domestic crude are bidding it up; refiners dependent on waterborne imports are constrained.

When the inversion normalizes, physical supply chains are healing. Until then, every oil analysis using Brent as reference understates the real cost for US refiners.

**3. The AI capex debt spiral.**

Hyperscalers are committing $660-690B in 2026 capex, spending 90% of operating cash flow (up from 65% in 2025). Morgan Stanley expects hyperscaler borrowing to top $400B, double 2025's $165B. Amazon's free cash flow is projected to turn negative. Oracle's capital intensity is at 57%.

80% of firms report zero AI productivity gains. Software -- 25-40% of private credit portfolios -- is in a structural bear market. The capex is being debt-financed at scale into rising rates, a war-driven energy shock, and no measurable return on investment. If productivity does not validate within 12-18 months, the debt-funded capex becomes a credit event.

## Software Is Now Cheaper Than the Market

SaaStr reported that software stocks now trade at a discount to the S&P 500 for the first time in the sector's history. IGV at $79 is down 24% YTD. Median SaaS valuation: 3-12x ARR. ServiceNow trades at $102 against a consensus target of $625.

Entry 2 argued this was structural: AI agents performing seat compression, 35% of enterprises replacing SaaS tools, per-seat pricing becoming obsolete. Seven weeks later:

- 126,000 tech layoffs in 2026 (815/day, accelerating)
- Oracle confirmed 20,000-30,000 cuts, the largest single tech layoff of the year
- Anthropic revenue: $9B (end 2025) to $19B (March 2026) -- doubled in three months
- Software trades at a discount to the S&P for the first time in history
- Nasdaq -11.4% YTD vs Russell 2000 Value +8.9% -- a 20-point gap not seen since the dot-com bust

Capital is leaving large-cap tech and flowing to small-cap value. AI compresses the revenue of the companies that justified growth multiples; the premium evaporates.

The forward mechanism for the software short is Q1 earnings (late April). At historic discounts, the market has priced a bad outcome. The question is whether guidance confirms the structural thesis (AI seat compression is permanent and accelerating) or whether bad results are "already priced in" and trigger mean reversion. If companies guide for continued seat compression and revenue deceleration, the discount is justified and deepens. If they stabilize, the short gets squeezed. The structural thesis (AI replacing seats) survives a Fed cut -- it is not cyclical. But at these valuations, the asymmetry has shifted: the next 10% down is harder than the first 20%. The position stays at 10% because the thesis is structural, not because the easy money remains.

Anthropic: $9B to $19B in three months. OpenAI at $25B ARR. Both planning IPOs. The value destroyed in software incumbents is being recreated in foundation model companies -- privately, inaccessible to public equity investors.
