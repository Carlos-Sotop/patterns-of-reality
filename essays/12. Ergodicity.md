In 2011, the physicist Ole Peters published a paper that challenged an assumption economics had relied on for nearly three centuries. The challenge did not involve new data, new mathematics, or new technology. It involved a coin flip.

The game is simple. A fair coin is flipped. Heads, your wealth increases by 50%. Tails, it decreases by 40%. You can play as many rounds as you like.

The expected value of each round is positive. Half the time you gain 50%, half the time you lose 40%. The average outcome per flip is a 5% gain. By the standard criteria of decision theory — maximize expected value — this is a good bet. You should play.

Peters asked what happens to a single person who actually plays this game over time.

After two rounds, one heads, one tails, in any order — your wealth is not 1.05 times 1.05. It is 1.5 times 0.6, which equals 0.9. You have lost 10%. The order does not matter: 0.6 times 1.5 is also 0.9. Each pair of flips — one gain, one loss — shrinks your wealth by 10%.

After a hundred rounds, with roughly fifty heads and fifty tails, your wealth is approximately 0.9 raised to the fiftieth power — about half a percent of what you started with. You have lost 99.5% of everything, playing a game with positive expected value the entire time.

The expected value across many people, each playing once, grows at 5% per round. The experienced outcome for one person playing many rounds shrinks at roughly 5% per round. These are not the same number. The difference is not a rounding error or a technicality. It is the difference between a statistic and a life.

## The Historical Error

The tension is older than Peters. In 1738, Daniel Bernoulli confronted a related puzzle, now called the St. Petersburg paradox: a game with infinite expected value that no rational person would pay much to play. Bernoulli's solution was to propose that people evaluate gains not by their absolute amount but by their proportion to existing wealth — logarithmic utility. A doubling matters less to a rich person than to a poor one.

This was mathematically productive. Expected utility theory, formalized by von Neumann and Morgenstern in 1944, became the foundation of modern decision theory, game theory, and financial economics. The framework works: specify a utility function, compute expected utility, choose the option that maximizes it. Three centuries of economic theory rest on this structure.

But Bernoulli's solution contained an invisible assumption. By recasting the problem as one of psychological preference — people *prefer* diminishing marginal returns — he framed logarithmic utility as a behavioral hypothesis about human risk attitudes. Some people are more risk-averse, some less. The logarithm was treated as one possible utility function among many, a description of subjective preference rather than an objective fact.

Peters' insight was that the logarithm is not a preference. It is a consequence of mathematics.

When outcomes are multiplicative — when gains and losses compound on your existing wealth — the time-average growth rate is the expected value of the logarithm of returns, not the logarithm of the expected value. These are different quantities. The first can be negative while the second is positive. The first describes what happens to a person living through time. The second describes what happens to an average across a population at a single moment.

Bernoulli stumbled onto the right answer for the wrong reason. He thought people *choose* to weight outcomes logarithmically because of diminishing sensitivity to wealth. Peters showed that any person who exists in time and faces multiplicative dynamics *must* evaluate outcomes this way — not as a preference, but as a condition of survival.

## Ensemble and Time

The distinction is precise.

An *ensemble average* is computed across many instances at one moment. Take a thousand people. Each flips the coin once. Average their outcomes. The result is a 5% gain. The ensemble grows.

A *time average* is computed across many moments for one instance. Take one person. They flip the coin a thousand times. Compute their growth rate. The result is approximately a 5% loss per round. The individual shrinks.

In many systems, these two averages are equal. The technical term is *ergodicity*: a process is ergodic if its time average converges to its ensemble average. For additive dynamics — where gains and losses are fixed amounts, not proportions — the process is ergodic and the distinction does not matter. If you gain $50 on heads and lose $40 on tails, the expected value and the time-average growth are both +$5 per round, regardless of whether you compute across people or across time.

But wealth dynamics are not additive. They are multiplicative. A 50% gain followed by a 40% loss does not return you to where you started. It leaves you at 90%. Each outcome scales the current state, and the compounding of proportional gains and losses produces a growth rate that diverges from the expected value.

This is the non-ergodicity of wealth. The ensemble average says the system is growing. The time average says the typical participant is shrinking. Both are mathematically correct. They are answers to different questions. The problem is that economics, for three centuries, has been answering the ensemble question and applying the result to individuals who live in time.

## The Absorbing State

Non-ergodicity becomes catastrophic in the presence of ruin.

In a multiplicative game, if your wealth ever reaches zero, the game is over. Zero times any positive number is zero. No future gain can restore you. In the language of probability theory, zero is an *absorbing state*: once entered, it cannot be left.

This changes everything. A game with positive expected value but the possibility of ruin is fundamentally different from the same game without ruin. The ensemble average — computed across all players, including those who were ruined — is dominated by the few lucky paths that avoided ruin and compounded gains. The majority of individual paths converge toward zero.

The effect is not limited to literal bankruptcy. Any boundary that permanently removes you from the game functions as an absorbing state. A margin call that forces liquidation at the worst moment. A job loss during a recession when no one is hiring. A health crisis without insurance. A business failure that destroys not just capital but reputation and relationships. In each case, the individual's path terminates or resets to a level from which recovery is structurally harder, while the ensemble average — GDP, market returns, employment rates — continues to grow.

This is why the Kelly criterion, derived by John Kelly at Bell Labs in 1956, specifies an optimal bet size that is always less than the amount that maximizes expected value. For the coin flip game, the Kelly-optimal fraction is 25% of wealth — not 100%. The Kelly bettor sacrifices expected return for growth-rate maximization, which is equivalent to time-average optimization. A person who bets the Kelly fraction will, over sufficient time, outgrow anyone who bets more or less, because the Kelly fraction is the one that avoids ruin while maximizing the rate at which wealth actually compounds.

The Kelly criterion was not derived from behavioral assumptions about risk preference. It was derived from information theory. It is the strategy that maximizes the long-run growth rate of wealth. The fact that it looks identical to Bernoulli's logarithmic utility — derived from completely different premises — is not coincidence. Both arrive at the same answer because both are responding to the same mathematical reality: multiplicative dynamics in time require logarithmic evaluation.

## The Behavioral Inversion

This reframes a century of behavioral economics.

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky documented that people systematically deviate from expected-value maximization. They overweight small probabilities, underweight large ones, are more sensitive to losses than gains, and refuse gambles with positive expected value. Kahneman and Tversky interpreted these as cognitive biases — systematic errors in human judgment, departures from the rational norm of expected utility maximization.

Peters' framework inverts this interpretation. If the rational norm is time-average optimization rather than ensemble-average maximization, then many of these "biases" are not errors at all. They are correct.

Loss aversion — caring more about a 40% loss than a 50% gain — is rational under multiplicative dynamics, because the loss compounds more destructively than the gain compounds constructively. The asymmetry is not in the person's psychology. It is in the mathematics.

Refusing a positive-expected-value gamble is rational when the gamble's time-average growth rate is negative. The person does not need to understand the Kelly criterion to behave as though they do. Evolutionary selection has the same mathematical structure as time-average optimization: lineages that survive are the ones that avoid ruin, not the ones that maximize expected offspring in a single generation.

Insurance has negative expected value — you pay more in premiums, on average, than you collect in claims. But insurance is rational under time-average optimization, because it eliminates the absorbing state. The expected-value thinker says insurance is irrational. The time-average thinker says it is the cost of remaining in the game.

The implication is that behavioral economics has spent decades pathologizing correct behavior. People are not irrational for refusing positive-expected-value bets. They are not biased for weighing losses more heavily than gains. They are solving the right problem — survival in time — while the theory against which they are measured solves the wrong one.

## Game Theory Under Mortality

Standard game theory, from Nash onwards, analyzes strategic interaction between players who either play once or play indefinitely. The one-shot game asks: what is the optimal strategy for a single interaction? The infinitely repeated game asks: what strategies are sustainable when the game never ends?

Neither framework accounts for what happens when players can be permanently eliminated.

In a one-shot prisoner's dilemma, defection is the dominant strategy. In an infinitely repeated version, cooperation can be sustained through tit-for-tat and similar strategies, because the threat of future punishment disciplines current behavior. But in a repeated game where ruin is possible — where a sufficiently bad outcome removes a player entirely — the strategic calculus changes in a way neither framework captures.

When elimination is possible, strategies that maximize expected payoff per round can lead to extinction. A player who always defects might accumulate more per round on average, but if even one round produces ruin — if a retaliatory coalition wipes out the defector — the strategy's time-average value is zero. The ensemble of all possible runs says defection wins. The time average, where the player must survive every single round, says cooperation is safer.

This is not the standard folk theorem argument for cooperation, which relies on the shadow of the future — the expectation that the game continues indefinitely and future punishment makes defection costly. The ergodicity argument is different and stronger: cooperation is optimal because it avoids the absorbing state. You do not cooperate because you fear punishment in the next round. You cooperate because the alternative includes paths to ruin, and any strategy that includes ruin has a time-average value of zero, regardless of how high its expected value is.

Evolutionary game theory has formalized this. Strategies that risk extinction are eliminated by selection even when they have higher expected reproductive value. The strategy that survives is not the one that reproduces fastest in expectation, but the one whose lineage avoids the absorbing state. Over sufficient time, selection for time-average fitness eliminates all strategies that maximize ensemble-average fitness at the cost of ruin risk.

The implication for system design: any system where participants can be permanently ruined selects for cooperative, ruin-avoiding behavior whether or not the participants understand the mathematics. The system's dynamics do the selecting. Design the system so that ruin states exist for exploitative behavior and not for cooperative behavior, and cooperation emerges without requiring altruism, enforcement, or even conscious choice.

## The Aggregate Illusion

The confusion between ensemble and time averages is not merely academic. It is the lens through which entire economies are evaluated, and the distortion is consequential.

Gross domestic product is an ensemble average. It sums all economic activity across all participants at a given moment. When GDP grows, the ensemble is expanding. But the individual experience — the time-average path of a specific worker, household, or community — can diverge from the ensemble for decades.

In the United States, real GDP per capita roughly doubled between 1980 and 2020. Real median household income grew by approximately 20% over the same period. Real wages for production workers were essentially flat. The ensemble doubled. The median participant gained modestly. The modal participant gained nothing.

This is not a distributional accident. It is the mathematical signature of non-ergodic dynamics. In a multiplicative system, the ensemble average is dominated by the upper tail — the few paths that compounded gains without hitting ruin — while most individual paths grow slower than the average, and some contract or terminate.

Financial markets exhibit the same structure. The average annual return of the S&P 500 over the past century is approximately 10%. But the average investor earns substantially less, because they enter and exit at the wrong times, face margin calls during drawdowns, and are forced to sell during crises precisely when the ensemble is cheapest. The ensemble return is a property of the index. The time-average return is a property of the participant, and the two diverge because the participant can be forced out of the game.

The Reflexivity essay described how the economic circuit — production generating the income that funds the consumption that sustains production — is breaking as AI displaces labor. The ensemble view of this transition is sanguine: total output rises, total productivity improves, the economy grows. But the time-average view asks: what happens to the individual whose income channel was severed? For that person, the transition is an absorbing state. No amount of aggregate growth compensates for a personal path that has hit zero.

Policy evaluated on ensemble metrics — GDP growth, average returns, total employment — is policy that optimizes for the statistician's perspective. It asks: is the average getting better? The ergodic question is: is each person's path through time getting better? These are different questions with different answers, and designing systems on the basis of the first while people live the second is the structural error that non-ergodicity reveals.

## The Series Through Ergodicity

Each essay in this series has described a system failure. Read through the lens of ergodicity, these failures share a common structure: systems optimized for ensemble performance that betray individuals living in time.

The Non-Delegation essay described how custodial intermediaries can freeze, seize, or revoke access. In ensemble terms, the banking system functions: the vast majority of accounts are accessible, the vast majority of transactions clear, the system's aggregate performance is excellent. But for the individual whose account is frozen — the Argentine during the corralito, the Canadian during the trucker protests, the Cypriot during the bail-in — the ensemble average is irrelevant. Their time-average path has hit an absorbing state. Self-custody is not a philosophical preference. It is ergodic insurance: the elimination of a ruin state from the individual's path.

The Criticality essay described how suppressing small corrections accumulates the fuel for large ones. This is ensemble optimization in its purest form: suppress volatility, smooth the average, maintain the appearance of stability. The ensemble looks calm. But the time-average cost is accumulating in a way the ensemble conceals. When the critical event arrives, the individual who was told the system was stable discovers that eighty years of suppressed corrections have loaded the next moment with catastrophic potential. Fire suppression optimized the ensemble of fire seasons while loading a particular season with ruin-level fuel.

The Degrees of Freedom essay described how regulation constrains known dimensions while the system evolves along unmodeled ones. Regulation is ensemble thinking: a rule that improves average outcomes across all participants. But individuals navigate the full dimensionality of the system, and constraints that improve the average can create ruin corridors along dimensions the regulator did not model. The Lucas critique — that policy changes behavior, invalidating the model the policy was based on — is an ergodicity problem: the ensemble model is static, but participants evolve in time.

The Reflexivity essay described how the economic circuit depends on labor occupying both sides — factor of production and source of demand. The ensemble view: output equals income equals expenditure. Everything balances. But the time-average view reveals that this balance depends on one mechanism, and if that mechanism fails for any subset of participants, their path diverges from the ensemble permanently. UBI patches attempt to restore the ensemble balance without addressing the time-average reality: each individual's path is now dependent on a single political channel that carries all the ruin risks the earlier essays cataloged.

The Intents essay described how systems optimized for aggregate efficiency — total volume, total revenue, total throughput — diverge from individual intent. This is the ensemble-time gap applied to purpose: the system maximizes an aggregate objective while individual participants, navigating their own paths through time, find that the system does not serve their specific needs. Intent-based architecture is the correction: optimize for each participant's path, not for the population's average.

## The Design Principle

Ergodicity provides a mathematical criterion for evaluating any system: does it optimize for the time average or the ensemble average?

A system that optimizes for the ensemble average can grow in aggregate while ruining its participants. A system that optimizes for the time average grows more slowly in aggregate but ensures that the growth is experienced by the people living through it.

The criterion is specific. A time-average-optimal system must:

**Avoid absorbing states.** No single event should be capable of permanently removing a participant from the system. Self-custody prevents institutional seizure. Multiple income channels prevent single-source dependency. Circuit breakers in financial systems prevent cascading liquidation. The network architecture of the Reflexivity essay — multiple paths, no single necessary channel — is the direct structural expression of this requirement.

**Make ruin states symmetric.** The game theory section showed that when ruin is possible, cooperation emerges as the dominant strategy — but only if the ruin state applies equally. If some participants can be ruined while others cannot, the game becomes exploitative. Intermediaries that can freeze your assets while their own assets remain protected violate this symmetry. Cryptographic self-custody and non-delegated systems restore it.

**Maintain optionality.** The Kelly criterion specifies not just how much to bet but how much to keep in reserve. The reserve is not idle capital — it is the option to participate in future rounds. Path dependence is the erosion of optionality: as a system locks in, the set of available paths narrows. Time-average-optimal design keeps paths open, because the value of being in the game tomorrow may exceed the value of any bet available today.

**Distribute risk across independent channels.** In portfolio theory, diversification reduces variance. In ergodic terms, it does something more fundamental: it prevents any single outcome from being an absorbing state. The network architecture is not merely economically efficient — it is ergodically necessary. A single channel of income, a single store of value, a single institutional relationship is a single path to ruin. Multiple channels are multiple paths to survival.

**Evaluate outcomes over paths, not moments.** Ensemble thinking evaluates a policy by its average effect across all affected people at one moment. Time-average thinking evaluates it by following each person's path through all future moments. The same policy that improves the ensemble today can degrade the time-average for individuals who will live through its compounding consequences. Systems designed for the time average must track individual trajectories, not population statistics.

## The Arithmetic

The essays in this series have argued for an architecture: decentralized, intent-aligned, network-structured, non-delegated, multi-channel. These arguments have been made on grounds of political resilience, institutional design, structural defense, system dynamics, mechanism alignment, transition positioning, and economic necessity.

Ergodicity provides the mathematical foundation that unifies them.

Every proposed design feature is a time-average optimization. Self-custody removes an absorbing state. Multiple income channels prevent single-source ruin. Intent-aligned systems serve individual paths rather than aggregate metrics. Network architectures ensure no single channel failure is catastrophic. Protocol-based cooperation makes ruin states symmetric. Each is derived not from political conviction but from the mathematics of survival in time.

The alternative — systems that optimize for the ensemble — is the architecture we have. GDP grows while individuals stagnate. Markets rise while participants are liquidated. Institutions are preserved while depositors are frozen. The aggregate looks healthy. The paths diverge.

Peters' coin flip is a thought experiment. But the structure it reveals is not. Every person navigating the modern economy faces multiplicative dynamics: gains and losses that compound, that scale with what you already have, that can — in a single bad outcome — change the trajectory of a life. The question is not whether the game has positive expected value. The question is whether the path through time is survivable.

The architecture this series describes is designed so that the answer is yes — not for the average participant, but for each one. Less elegant than an optimized ensemble. More robust than any single path. The kind of structure that survives not because it maximizes the mean but because it respects the individual. Not because it produces the best outcome on average but because it prevents the worst outcome in time.

That is what it means to design for ergodicity. Not to optimize for the world as a statistician sees it, but for the world as a person lives it.

**Notes and Definitions:**

*Ergodicity*: A property of a stochastic process in which the time average of a single trajectory converges to the ensemble average across many trajectories. In ergodic systems, what is true for the group is true for the individual over time. Many economically important processes — particularly those involving multiplicative dynamics of wealth — are non-ergodic: the ensemble average grows while typical individual paths shrink. The concept originates in statistical mechanics, where Ludwig Boltzmann introduced the ergodic hypothesis to justify computing macroscopic properties from ensemble averages.

*Ensemble average*: The expected value computed across many instances of a process at a single moment. In the coin flip game, the ensemble average of wealth across a thousand players after one flip is 1.05 times the starting wealth. This average can diverge arbitrarily from the time average experienced by any individual player.

*Time average*: The growth rate experienced by a single instance of a process across many periods. In the coin flip game, the time-average growth rate is approximately −5% per period — the geometric mean of the multiplicative outcomes — even though the ensemble average is +5%. For multiplicative processes, the time average equals the expected value of the logarithm of outcomes.

*St. Petersburg paradox*: A thought experiment proposed by Nicolas Bernoulli in 1713 and analyzed by Daniel Bernoulli in 1738. A coin is flipped until it lands tails. The payoff is 2ⁿ dollars, where n is the number of consecutive heads. The expected payoff is infinite, yet no rational person would pay a large amount to play. Bernoulli resolved the paradox by proposing logarithmic utility — people value proportional gains, not absolute ones — but framed this as a psychological preference rather than a mathematical necessity of time-average optimization.

*Kelly criterion*: A formula derived by John Kelly at Bell Labs in 1956 that specifies the optimal fraction of wealth to wager in a repeated gamble in order to maximize the long-run growth rate. The criterion is equivalent to maximizing the expected logarithm of wealth, which is equivalent to time-average optimization in multiplicative dynamics. The derivation was originally motivated by information theory — specifically, the problem of a gambler with access to a noisy information channel — not by behavioral assumptions about risk preferences.

*Absorbing state*: In probability theory, a state that, once entered, cannot be left. In economic contexts, bankruptcy or permanent exclusion from a system functions as an absorbing state: zero wealth multiplied by any return remains zero. The existence of absorbing states is what makes multiplicative processes non-ergodic — paths that hit the absorbing state are permanently removed from the game, but the ensemble average continues as though all paths remain viable.

*Loss aversion*: The empirical finding, documented by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in prospect theory (1979), that people weigh losses approximately twice as heavily as equivalent gains. Traditionally interpreted as a cognitive bias — a deviation from rational expected-value maximization. Under Peters' framework, loss aversion is rational behavior for an agent facing multiplicative dynamics, because losses compound more destructively than equivalent gains compound constructively.

*Expected utility theory*: A framework for decision-making under uncertainty, formalized by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern in *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior* (1944). Agents are modeled as maximizing the expected value of a utility function over outcomes. The framework assumes that the correct way to evaluate a gamble is to average outcomes weighted by their probabilities — an ensemble calculation — which is valid only if the process is ergodic.

*Folk theorem*: A result in game theory stating that in infinitely repeated games, any outcome that gives each player at least their minimax payoff can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium. The theorem is used to explain how cooperation can emerge in repeated strategic interactions. The standard proof assumes the game continues indefinitely; when players can be eliminated, the set of viable strategies narrows further to those that avoid ruin.
